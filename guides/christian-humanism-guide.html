Chapter 1 — Foundations of Ethics

4 Key Terms

Core Concepts

  • End / Intention — The purpose or goal toward which an action is directed. Every human act has an end — the reason why we act. The end gives meaning and moral direction to what we do.
  • Means — The actions or methods used to achieve a particular end. The moral quality of the means matters independently of the end — "the end does not justify the means."
  • First Principle — The foundational moral truth: "Do good and avoid evil." Known through synderesis (the innate moral sense); the starting point of all moral reasoning.
  • Ethics — The branch of philosophy that studies human actions in terms of their moral goodness or badness. Asks: "What should I do?" and "What makes an action right or wrong?"

Key Relationships

ConceptDefinitionKey Point
EndGoal / purpose of an actionThe "why" — gives moral direction
MeansMethods used to reach the endThe "how" — must also be morally good
First Principle"Do good and avoid evil"Self-evident; known through synderesis
EthicsStudy of moral goodness/badness of actsPractical — about what we SHOULD do
⚠️ Exam Trap: A good end does NOT justify immoral means. Both must be evaluated separately.

Chapter 2 — Happiness

10 Key Terms

Core Concepts

  • Happiness — Complete fulfillment from achieving one's ultimate end. NOT mere pleasure — it is the fulfillment of human nature.
  • Human Nature — Rational, social, free, and oriented toward the good. Understanding human nature is essential to understanding true happiness.
  • Good — That which fulfills or perfects a being according to its nature.
  • Goods of the Body — Health, strength, physical pleasure, life. Real goods, but lower in the hierarchy.
  • Goods of the Soul — Wisdom, virtue, love, friendship, knowledge of truth. Higher goods — they perfect our rational nature.
  • Hierarchy of Goods — Soul goods rank above body goods. The highest good is the Beatific Vision (union with God).
  • Ultimate End — The final goal that, once achieved, leaves nothing more to be desired. In Christian Humanism: God / the Beatific Vision.
  • Substitutes for Happiness — Wealth, pleasure, power, fame, honor. These are finite goods that cannot satisfy our infinite desire.
  • Beatific Vision — Direct encounter with God in heaven — the only source of perfect, lasting happiness because only God is infinite.
  • Nature — The essence of a thing — what makes it what it is.

Hierarchy of Goods (Lowest → Highest)

LevelTypeExamplesWhy Limited
1 (lowest)SubstitutesWealth, fame, pleasure, powerFinite; cannot satisfy infinite desire
2Goods of the BodyHealth, strength, lifePhysical — serve the soul's goods
3Goods of the SoulWisdom, virtue, love, truthHigh, but still created goods
4 (highest)Beatific VisionUnion with GodInfinite — perfectly fulfills us
💡 Only the infinite (God) can satisfy an infinite desire. Finite goods always leave us wanting more.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Happiness ≠ Pleasure. Pleasure is temporary feeling; happiness is lasting fulfillment of nature.

Chapter 3 — Law

18 Key Terms

Definition of Law

A valid law requires all four elements:

Law = Ordinance of Reason + For the Common Good + By Legitimate Authority + Promulgated

Types of Law (Hierarchy)

TypeSourceKnown ThroughKey Feature
Eternal LawGod's plan for all creationFaith / Reason (partially)Universal, unchanging
Natural LawHuman reason participating in Eternal LawReason aloneWritten on the heart; 1st precept: "Do good, avoid evil"
Divine LawGod's revealed law (Scripture)RevelationTen Commandments; supplements Natural Law
Human/Civil LawHuman lawmakersLegislationMust not contradict Natural or Divine Law

Rights and Duties

  • Right — A moral claim one person has on another. Every right implies a corresponding duty.
  • Duty — A moral obligation to respect others' rights. Rights and duties are correlative.
  • Subject of a Right — The person who possesses the right (rights-holder).
  • Term of a Right — The person who has the duty corresponding to that right (duty-bearer).
  • Matter of a Right — The specific thing the right concerns (life, property, speech, etc.).

Freedom

TypeDefinitionMoral Status
Genuine FreedomThe ability to choose the goodTrue freedom — ordered toward truth and goodness
Absolute FreedomDoing anything without restrictionFalse — a distortion of true freedom
⚠️ Exam Trap: Natural Law vs. Divine Law — Natural Law is known through REASON; Divine Law requires REVELATION (Scripture).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Absolute Freedom ≠ Genuine Freedom. Absolute = do anything (false); Genuine = choose the good (true).

Chapter 4 — Conscience

15 Key Terms

What Conscience Is (and Isn't)

ConscienceConsciousness
A judgment of the practical intellect about the moral goodness/badness of a specific actionAwareness of one's own existence and surroundings
Rational — NOT a feelingNOT a moral judgment
⚠️ #1 Exam Trap: Conscience ≠ Consciousness. These are completely different things.

Two Types of Intellect

Practical IntellectSpeculative Intellect
Directed toward action — what to DODirected toward knowing truth for its own sake — what IS
Conscience operates hereAbstract knowledge lives here

Moral Reasoning (Syllogism)

Major Premise: "Stealing is wrong." (general principle) Minor Premise: "Taking this wallet is stealing." (specific case) Conclusion: "Taking this wallet is wrong." (judgment of conscience)

Types of Conscience / Ignorance

TypeDefinitionMoral Guilt?
Invincible IgnoranceCannot be overcome even with reasonable effort❌ NO — person genuinely could not have known
Vincible IgnoranceCAN be overcome but hasn't been✅ YES — person should have known better
Erroneous ConscienceMakes a wrong moral judgmentDepends on invincible vs. vincible
Doubtful ConscienceCannot reach a firm judgmentMust NOT act until doubt resolved

Synderesis

Synderesis — The innate, natural disposition to know first moral principles ("Do good and avoid evil"). It is:

  • Innate — present from birth, not learned
  • Indestructible — cannot be lost or removed
  • The foundation of all moral reasoning
🔑 Rule: Never act with a doubtful conscience — resolve the doubt first through prayer, study, or counsel.

Chapter 5 — Human Acts

15 Key Terms

Human Acts vs. Acts of Man

Human ActAct of Man
Performed WITH knowledge and free willPerformed WITHOUT knowledge and/or free will
CAN be morally evaluated (good or bad)Cannot be morally evaluated
Examples: choosing to help, stealing, prayingExamples: breathing, digesting, reflex actions
⚠️ Exam Trap: Human Act ≠ Act of Man. Only human acts (deliberate choices) are subject to moral evaluation.

Components of a Human Act

  • Knowledge — Awareness of what you're doing
  • Free Will — The ability to choose otherwise
  • Deliberation — Considering options before choosing
  • Intention — The purpose or goal in mind
  • Consent — The will accepting a course of action

Interior vs. Exterior Acts

Interior ActExterior Act
Occurs within the mind/will (thoughts, intentions)Physical, observable action in the world
Has moral value even if never expressed externallyThe visible expression of interior acts

Passion and Moral Responsibility

TypeTimingEffect on Guilt
Antecedent PassionArises BEFORE a decision⬇️ Diminishes responsibility — clouded judgment
Consequent PassionArises AFTER a decision (willed by you)➡️ Does NOT diminish — may even increase
⚠️ Exam Trap: Antecedent passion REDUCES guilt (emotion came first). Consequent passion does NOT (you chose to fuel it).

What Diminishes Voluntariness

  1. Ignorance — Invincible ignorance removes guilt; vincible does not
  2. Passion — Antecedent diminishes; consequent does not
  3. Habit — Can make acts less conscious, but you're still responsible for forming the habit

Chapter 6 — Morality of Human Acts

20 Key Terms

The Three Sources of Morality

For an act to be MORALLY GOOD: Object ✓ + End/Intention ✓ + Circumstances ✓ (ALL three must be good)
SourceWhat It IsKey Rule
ObjectWHAT is being done (the core action)Primary determinant — some objects are intrinsically evil
End / IntentionWHY the person is doing itGood end ≠ justify evil means
CircumstancesContext: who, what, where, when, how muchCan modify gravity but CANNOT make intrinsically evil acts good
⚠️ Intrinsically Evil Acts (murder, lying, adultery, etc.) are ALWAYS wrong — no intention or circumstance can justify them.

Proximate vs. Remote End

Proximate EndRemote End
The immediate, closest "why"The further, bigger picture "why" behind the proximate end

Physical Evil vs. Moral Evil

Physical EvilMoral Evil
Natural suffering: disease, disastersEvil from free human choice against moral law
NOT caused by human moral choicesSin is moral evil

Principle of Double Effect

When an action has BOTH good and bad effects, ALL FOUR conditions must be met:

  1. The act itself must be morally good or neutral (not intrinsically evil)
  2. The good effect must be intended; the bad effect only tolerated (not desired)
  3. The good effect must NOT come through the bad effect (bad effect is not the means)
  4. There must be proportionate reason — the good must outweigh the bad
💡 Classic example: A surgeon removes a cancerous organ, knowing the patient will lose that function. The bad (loss of organ) is tolerated, not intended, and the good (saving life) proportionately outweighs it.

Types of Cooperation with Evil

TypeDefinitionAlways Wrong?
FormalShare in the evil intention — you approve and intend the wrongdoing✅ YES — always wrong
MaterialContribute to evil act WITHOUT sharing evil intention❌ May be permissible (evaluate with Double Effect)
ImmediateDirectly participate in the evil act itselfUsually wrong; very hard to justify
MediateContribute to circumstances that make evil possibleEasier to justify than immediate

⚠️ Exam Trap: Formal cooperation = share the evil INTENT (always wrong). Material cooperation = contribute without sharing intent (may be OK).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Sins of omission are real sins. Failing to act when you should is morally culpable.
