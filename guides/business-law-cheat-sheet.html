Sources of Law (Hierarchy)
SourceDescription
Constitutional LawSupreme law of the land; federal > state constitutions
Statutory LawLaws passed by legislatures (Congress, state legislatures)
Administrative LawRules/regulations from federal agencies (EPA, OSHA, FTC)
Common LawJudge-made law; based on precedent and case decisions
Treaty LawInternational agreements; supreme over state law
Hierarchy: Constitution → Federal Statute → Federal Regulation → State Constitution → State Statute → Common Law
Common Law & Stare Decisis
ConceptDefinition
Stare Decisis"Stand by the decision" — courts follow prior rulings (precedent)
PrecedentPrior court ruling used to decide similar future cases
Binding PrecedentMust be followed (decisions from higher courts in same jurisdiction)
Persuasive PrecedentNot binding but may influence (other circuits, law review articles)
OverruleHigher court rejects its own prior ruling
DistinguishCourt finds material facts different → doesn't apply precedent
EquityFairness-based legal decisions; injunctions, specific performance
Federal Court Structure
LevelCourtRole
TrialU.S. District Courts94 courts; original jurisdiction; hear evidence & facts
AppellateU.S. Courts of Appeals13 circuits; review errors of law (no new evidence)
HighestU.S. Supreme Court9 justices; certiorari; final word on federal law & Constitution
State Courts (typical)
Trial CourtsGeneral & limited jurisdiction trial courts
IntermediateState Courts of Appeals
HighestState Supreme Court (final on state law)
Jurisdiction
TypeMeaning
Subject Matter JurisdictionCourt's authority to hear a type of case (federal Q, diversity)
Personal JurisdictionCourt's authority over the parties in the lawsuit
In PersonamJurisdiction over a person (served process in state)
In RemJurisdiction over property located in the state
Diversity JurisdictionParties from different states; amount > $75,000 → federal court
Minimum ContactsDefendant must have purposeful contacts with forum state (Int'l Shoe)
Long-Arm StatuteState law allowing courts to reach out-of-state defendants
VenueProper geographic location within the court system
Key case: International Shoe Co. v. Washington — established "minimum contacts" standard for personal jurisdiction
Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
MethodHow It WorksBinding?
NegotiationParties resolve dispute directly, no third partyNo (unless contract)
MediationNeutral mediator facilitates discussion; no decision❌ No
ArbitrationNeutral arbitrator hears evidence and decides✅ Usually yes
Mini-TrialAbbreviated proceeding; executives try to settleNo
Summary Jury TrialNon-binding mock trial to gauge outcome❌ No
Arbitration clauses in contracts are enforceable (FAA — Federal Arbitration Act). Mandatory arbitration = common in employment & consumer contracts.
Legal Reasoning & IRAC Method
StepWhat to Do
I — IssueIdentify the legal question being decided
R — RuleState the applicable law or legal standard
A — ApplicationApply the rule to the facts of the case
C — ConclusionState the legal outcome/answer
Types of Legal Reasoning
DeductiveRule → apply to facts → conclusion (syllogism)
InductiveFacts → pattern → general rule
AnalogicalCompare current facts to prior case; apply same rule
Policy ReasoningDecide based on social goals or fairness
