✝️ Christian Humanism — Cheat Sheet

Procadamia | Key Concepts, Definitions & Moral Framework
Ch. 1 — Foundations of Ethics
TermDefinition
EthicsBranch of philosophy studying the moral goodness/badness of human actions
End / IntentionThe purpose or goal toward which an action is directed (the "why")
MeansThe actions or methods used to achieve an end (the "how")
First Principle"Do good and avoid evil" — foundational moral truth, known through synderesis
SynderesisThe innate moral sense; the natural disposition to know the first principle
⚠️ The end does NOT justify the means — both must be morally good
Ch. 2 — Happiness (Beatitude)
TermDefinition
BeatitudePerfect happiness; ultimate end of human existence (union with God)
EudaimoniaAristotle's term for flourishing / human happiness through virtue
Imperfect HappinessNatural happiness achievable in this life (health, virtue, friendship)
Perfect HappinessSupernatural happiness; vision of God (beatific vision); only in eternal life
BonumThe Good — that which all things seek by their nature
Aquinas: Humans are oriented toward God as their ultimate end (telos); earthly goods are good but insufficient for perfect happiness
Ch. 3 — The Four Types of Law
TypeDefinition
Eternal LawGod's rational governance of all creation; the source of all other law
Natural LawHuman reason's participation in eternal law; universally knowable moral truths
Divine (Positive) LawRevealed law (Scripture, Ten Commandments, New Testament); guides to eternal life
Human Positive LawLaws created by human authority; just only if in accord with natural law
Hierarchy: Eternal → Natural → Divine → Human. An unjust human law is no law at all (Aquinas)
"An unjust law is no law at all." — Augustine / Aquinas
Ch. 4 — Conscience
TermDefinition
ConscienceThe practical judgment of reason about the morality of a concrete act
Certain ConscienceFirm, well-reasoned moral judgment — must always be followed
Doubtful ConscienceUncertainty about morality — one must resolve doubt before acting
Erroneous ConscienceMistaken moral judgment; vincible (culpable) vs. invincible (not culpable)
Vincible IgnoranceCould have known better; morally culpable
Invincible IgnoranceCouldn't have known better; not morally culpable
FormationDuty to educate/form one's conscience using reason, Scripture, and Church teaching
One must always follow a certain conscience, even if objectively wrong — but has a duty to form conscience correctly
Ch. 5 — Human Acts
TermDefinition
Human ActAct performed knowingly and freely — subject to moral evaluation
Act of ManAct done without knowledge or free will (reflex, sleep, instinct) — not morally evaluated
KnowledgeMust know what one is doing for it to be a human act
FreedomMust act voluntarily (without compulsion) for moral responsibility
VoluntarinessDegree to which an act is freely chosen
Impediments to Human Acts
IgnoranceLack of knowledge; reduces/removes responsibility
PassionStrong emotion; antecedent passion reduces freedom; consequent does not
FearCan diminish freedom; grave fear can reduce culpability
Violence / ForceExternal compulsion; removes voluntariness entirely
Ch. 6 — Morality of Human Acts
The Three Fonts of MoralityDescription
ObjectThe act itself — what is done (intrinsic moral quality)
Intention / EndWhy the act is done — the agent's purpose
CircumstancesWho, what, when, where, how, and effects — can increase or decrease morality
Key Principles
Intrinsic EvilActs evil by their object regardless of intention (murder, lying under oath)
Double EffectA single act with 2 effects (good + bad) may be permitted if: act is good/neutral; bad effect not the means; bad not intended; proportionate reason
CooperationFormal = shares evil intention (always wrong); Material = provides assistance (may be permitted)
⚠️ For an act to be morally good: ALL THREE fonts must be good. One defect makes the act morally deficient.
Core Principles at a Glance
  • First Principle: "Do good and avoid evil"
  • End ≠ justifies means: both end AND means must be good
  • Four types of law: Eternal → Natural → Divine → Human
  • Unjust law: has no binding moral force (Augustine/Aquinas)
  • Conscience: always follow certain conscience; duty to form it correctly
  • Human act = knowledge + freedom (both required for moral evaluation)
  • Three fonts: Object + Intention + Circumstances — ALL must be good
  • Intrinsic evils are always wrong regardless of intention
  • Double effect: 4 conditions must ALL be met
  • Beatitude = ultimate end; only God fully satisfies human longing
Key thinkers: Aristotle (eudaimonia, virtue ethics) | Thomas Aquinas (natural law, four types of law, three fonts) | Augustine (unjust law, beatitude)
