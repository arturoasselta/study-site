Describing Motion

Definitions
ScalarMagnitude only (e.g. speed, distance, time)
VectorMagnitude + direction (e.g. velocity, displacement)
DistanceTotal path length traveled — scalar (m)
Displacement (Δd)Straight-line change in position — vector (m). Δd = d_f − d_i
SpeedDistance / time — scalar (m/s)
VelocityDisplacement / time — vector (m/s)

Formulas
Average velocity
v_avg = Δd / Δt
= (d_f − d_i) / (t_f − t_i)
Average speed
speed = total distance / total time
Graph Interpretations
d-t graph slope= velocity (steep = fast, flat = stopped)
v-t graph slope= acceleration
v-t graph area= displacement
🔑 Vector addition: Draw displacement vectors tip-to-tail. The resultant goes from start to final tip. For perpendicular vectors: |R| = √(A² + B²), angle = arctan(B/A).

Vectors & Projectile Motion
~8 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions
Vector Components
x-component
A_x = A · cos θ
y-component
A_y = A · sin θ
Magnitude from components
|A| = √(A_x² + A_y²)
Direction angle
θ = arctan(A_y / A_x)
Projectile Motion Rules
Horizontal (x)Constant velocity: a_x = 0
x = v_x · t, where v_x = v_i · cos θ
Vertical (y)Constant acceleration: a_y = −g
v_iy = v_i · sin θ
IndependenceHorizontal and vertical motions are completely independent — solve separately!
Projectile Key Points
At max height (apex)
v_y = 0
v_x remains constant throughout flight
Hang time
use vertical: 2 × (v_iy / g)
Range
x = v_x · t_hang
Launched horizontally
v_iy = 0; only v_x initial
🎯 Projectile problem strategy: (1) Break initial velocity into v_x and v_y components. (2) Use vertical kinematics to find time. (3) Use horizontal kinematics to find range or position. Time is the link between x and y!
Ch 5
Forces
~10 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions
Newton's Three Laws
1st Law — InertiaAn object stays at rest or in constant motion unless a NET force acts on it. If F_net = 0 → a = 0.
2nd Law — F = maNet force = mass × acceleration. Direction of acceleration = direction of net force. Units: N = kg·m/s²
3rd Law — Action-ReactionFor every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces act on DIFFERENT objects.
Key Forces & Formulas
Net Force
F_net = ma
[N = kg·m/s²]
Weight
F_W = mg
g = 9.8 m/s²; weight acts downward
Kinetic Friction
f_k = μ_k · N
Opposes motion; μ_k always < μ_s
Static Friction (max)
f_s ≤ μ_s · N
Before object moves; can be less than max
Inclines & Special Cases
Incline: parallel force
F_∥ = mg · sin θ
Acts down the slope
Incline: perp. force
F_⊥ = mg · cos θ = N
Normal force on frictionless incline
Elevator: going up/accel
F_N = m(g + a)
Feel heavier (apparent weight > real)
Elevator: going down/accel
F_N = m(g − a)
Feel lighter; F_N = 0 in free fall (weightless)
📊 Free Body Diagrams: Always draw ALL forces on the object — Weight (down), Normal (⊥ surface), Friction (opposing motion), Applied forces, Tension. F_net = sum of ALL forces.
🚀 Terminal velocity: When drag force = gravity (F_drag = F_W), acceleration = 0 and speed is constant. Air resistance increases with speed until equilibrium.
Ch 6
Work, Energy & Power
~10 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions
Formulas
Work
W = F · d · cos θ
θ = angle between force & displacement. Units: Joules (J = N·m). Zero work if F ⊥ d (θ = 90°)
Kinetic Energy
KE = ½mv²
Energy of motion [J]. Doubles when v doubles? → KE × 4!
Gravitational PE
PE_g = mgh
h = height above reference [J]
Spring PE (elastic)
PE_s = ½kx²
k = spring constant (N/m), x = compression/stretch
Work-Energy Theorem
W_net = ΔKE = KE_f − KE_i
Net work done = change in KE
Power
P = W / t = F · v
Units: Watts (W = J/s)
Conservation of Energy
No friction (ideal)
KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f
Total mechanical energy stays constant
With friction
KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f + Q
Q = heat/internal energy lost to friction
Mechanical Energy
ME = KE + PE
Forms of Energy
Kinetic (KE) Gravitational PE Elastic PE (springs) Thermal/Internal Chemical Nuclear Electrical
💡 Energy conservation strategy: Set PE = 0 at the lowest point. At the top: KE = 0 (if starting from rest), PE = mgh. At the bottom: PE = 0, all energy is KE. Use KE_top + PE_top = KE_bottom + PE_bottom.
Ch 7
Momentum & Impulse
~8 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions
Formulas
Momentum
p = mv
Vector! Units: kg·m/s
Impulse
J = F · Δt = Δp = mΔv
Units: N·s = kg·m/s
Conservation of Momentum
p_before = p_after
m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v₁' + m₂v₂'
Perfectly Inelastic
(m₁+m₂)v_f = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Objects stick together after collision
Collision Types
Elastic CollisionMomentum AND kinetic energy both conserved. Objects bounce. (e.g., billiard balls)
Inelastic CollisionMomentum conserved; KE NOT fully conserved — some lost as heat/sound/deformation.
Perfectly InelasticObjects stick together. Maximum KE loss. Momentum still conserved.
ExplosionObjects start together, fly apart. Total momentum stays = 0 if initially at rest.
🚗 Airbags & crumple zones: Increase the time (Δt) of impact → same impulse (Δp) over longer time → smaller force (F = Δp/Δt). This is why longer stop times = smaller forces = safer crashes.

Gravity
6–10 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions (shared with UCM)
Universal Gravitation
Law of Universal Gravitation
F_g = G · m₁·m₂ / r²
G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² (Universal Gravitational Constant — Big G)
Inverse Square Law
If r × 2 → F_g ÷ 4
Double distance = force becomes ¼. Triple → 1/9
Gravitational field strength
g = F_g / m = GM / r²
Units: m/s² = N/kg; varies by planet/altitude
Weight from gravity
F_W = mg
g ≈ 9.8 m/s² at Earth's surface
Key People & Concepts
NewtonFormulated universal gravitation law. Gravity is a force between any two masses.
CavendishFirst measured Big G experimentally using a torsion balance (1798).
Gravitational field linesPoint toward the mass; stronger (closer together) near the mass surface.
Difference: g vs GLittle g = local acceleration (9.8 m/s² on Earth). Big G = universal constant (6.67×10⁻¹¹).
U9b
Uniform Circular Motion (UCM)
6–10 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions (shared with Gravity)
Formulas
UCM Speed
v = 2πr / T
T = period (time for 1 revolution)
Centripetal acceleration
a_c = v² / r
Always directed toward center (centripetal = "center-seeking")
Centripetal force
F_c = ma_c = mv² / r
NOT a new force — it's the NET inward force
Vector Directions & What Provides F_c
VelocityTangent to the circle (always perpendicular to radius). Changes direction constantly.
Acceleration & ForceAlways point toward the center. If the inward force stops → object moves in a straight line (tangent).
Gravity provides F_cPlanets orbiting the sun, satellites orbiting Earth — gravity acts as the centripetal force.
Tension provides F_cBall on a string swung in a horizontal circle.
Normal force provides F_cCar going over a hill or through a loop-the-loop (inside top).
Friction provides F_cCar turning on a flat road.
⭕ Key insight: Centripetal force is NOT a separate force — it's the name we give to whatever net inward force keeps the object in a circle. Identify which existing force (tension, gravity, normal, friction) is acting inward, set it equal to mv²/r, and solve.
U10
Fluids
6–10 multiple choice + 2 calculation questions
Hydrostatics (Fluids at Rest)
Density
ρ = m / V [kg/m³]
Water: ρ = 1000 kg/m³; Air: ρ ≈ 1.2 kg/m³
Pressure
P = F / A [Pa = N/m²]
Gauge pressure
P_gauge = ρgh
Pressure due to fluid depth only
Absolute pressure
P = P_atm + ρgh
P_atm ≈ 101,325 Pa ≈ 101.3 kPa
Buoyant force (Archimedes)
F_b = ρ_fluid · V_sub · g
= weight of fluid displaced
Floating condition
F_b = F_weight → ρ_obj < ρ_fluid
Pascal's Principle & Hydraulics
Pascal's Principle
Pressure transmitted equally throughout closed fluid
Hydraulics
F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂
Small force on small area → large force on large area
Hydrodynamics (Fluids in Motion)
Volume flow rate
Q = A · v [m³/s]
Mass flow rate
ṁ = ρ · A · v [kg/s]
Continuity Equation
A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
Narrow pipe → faster fluid (same flow rate)
Bernoulli's Equation
P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant
Faster flow = lower pressure (Bernoulli effect)
Torricelli's Theorem
v = √(2gh)
Speed of fluid exiting a hole at depth h
🌊 Bernoulli intuition: Faster-moving fluid has LOWER pressure. This explains airplane lift (faster air over curved top → lower pressure above wing), carburetors, and the curve of a spinning baseball. Continuity: when a pipe narrows, the fluid MUST speed up to maintain the same flow rate.
🪣 Archimedes' key idea: Whether an object floats or sinks depends entirely on whether ρ_object > or < ρ_fluid. The buoyant force = weight of fluid displaced (not weight of object). Even a heavy steel ship floats because its average density (hull + air inside) < water.
