Unit 1: Tort Law Overview & Intentional Torts to Persons

Tort: A civil (not criminal) wrongdoing that injures another person and creates legal liability.

Intentional Torts to Persons

  • Assault: Intentional act creating reasonable apprehension of immediate harmful contact (no actual contact required)
  • Battery: Intentional physical contact that is harmful or offensive
  • False Imprisonment: Intentional confinement without legal authority or consent
  • Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: Extreme conduct intentionally causing severe emotional distress
  • Defamation: False statement damaging someone's reputation (Libel = written; Slander = spoken)
  • Invasion of Privacy: Unauthorized intrusion into private affairs

Unit 2: Intentional Torts to Property

Trespass to Land & Chattels

  • Trespass to Land: Unauthorized entry onto another's property
  • Trespass to Chattels: Intentional interference with personal property
  • Conversion: Exercising dominion over property so serious it warrants full value recovery

Key: Conversion is more serious than trespass to chattels; allows recovery of full property value.

Unit 3: Negligence Elements (CRITICAL)

  1. Duty of Care: Defendant owed a legal duty to plaintiff
  2. Breach of Duty: Defendant failed to meet standard of care (reasonable person test)
  3. Actual Causation: "But-for" the breach, injury wouldn't have occurred
  4. Proximate Causation: Injury was foreseeable result of breach
  5. Damages: Plaintiff suffered actual harm (physical, emotional, property)

All 5 elements must be proven for negligence liability.

Landmark Case: Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad

Issue: Did the railroad owe a duty to Palsgraf (not the person who fell)?
Holding: NO. Duty extends only to foreseeable victims. Palsgraf was not in the foreseeable zone of danger.
Principle: Proximate causation limits liability to reasonably foreseeable consequences.

Unit 4: Causation & Damages

Actual vs. Proximate Causation

  • Actual Causation: "But-for" the breach, would injury occur? (factual causation)
  • Proximate Causation: Is the injury a foreseeable result? (legal causation)

Damages Types

  • Compensatory: Medical expenses, lost wages, pain/suffering, property damage
  • Punitive: Extra damages to punish egregious conduct (rare in negligence)
  • Nominal: Small amount when liability exists but no harm

Unit 5: Business Ethics & Corporate Responsibility

  • Utilitarianism: Maximize benefit for greatest number
  • Deontological Ethics: Focus on duties and rules
  • Corporate Stakeholders: Shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers, community, environment
  • Conflicts of Interest: Personal interests vs. company/client interests (require disclosure)

Unit 6: Constitutional Law & Commerce Clause

  • Commerce Clause: Congress can regulate interstate/foreign commerce and activities substantially affecting it
  • Commercial Speech: Protected but can be regulated (no false/misleading claims)
  • Federal Agencies: FDA, FTC, SEC, OSHA, EPA

Unit 7: Court Procedures & Civil Litigation

Jurisdiction

  • Subject Matter Jurisdiction: Court's power to hear TYPE of case
  • Personal Jurisdiction: Court's power over DEFENDANT
  • Venue: Proper geographic location for trial

Litigation Phases

  1. Pleadings (Complaint & Answer)
  2. Discovery (documents, depositions, interrogatories)
  3. Motions (dismiss, summary judgment)
  4. Trial (jury or judge)
  5. Judgment & Appeal

Unit 8: Appeal Process & Legal Terminology

Standards of Review

  • De Novo Review: Appellate court reviews legal questions independently
  • Abuse of Discretion: Only reverses if trial judge acted unreasonably
  • Clearly Erroneous: Reversed only if entire record shows clear conviction of wrong

Key Terms

  • Plaintiff, Defendant, Appellant, Appellee
  • Liability, Tort, Damages, Breach
  • Negligence, Proximate Cause, Precedent, Injunction
  • Statute of Limitations, Deposition, Summary Judgment

Unit 9: Landmark Cases Summary

Palsgraf v. Long Island Railroad: Duty extends only to foreseeable victims
May v. Chrysler Group LLC: Manufacturers owe duty to design safe products
Bad Frog Brewery v. NYS Liquor Authority: Government can regulate offensive commercial speech

Unit 10: Exam Prep - IRAC Method

Use IRAC to analyze every scenario:

  1. Issue: What is the legal question? (e.g., "Is the defendant liable for negligence?")
  2. Rule: What law applies? (e.g., "Negligence requires duty, breach, causation, damages")
  3. Application: How does law apply to facts? (e.g., "The defendant owed a duty to the plaintiff as a driver to other road users...")
  4. Conclusion: What is the result? (e.g., "The defendant is liable for negligence and owes $X in damages")

Exam Tips

  • Read all facts carefully—detail matters
  • Analyze every element, not just the first
  • Consider defenses even if defendant seems liable
  • Apply law to facts (don't just state the law)
  • Reach clear conclusions: Who is liable? For what? What damages?
